``` Detection and Characterization of Subsurface Ice in Lunar South Polar Regions Using Chandrayaan-2 Radar and Imagery Data for Landing Site and Rover Traverse Planning

Description: 
The discovery and characterization of water-ice in the lunar South Polar Region is a high-priority scientific and exploration objective, particularly for enabling sustained human presence on the Moon. Observations from Chandrayaan-2 have opened new avenues to probe surface/subsurface using high-resolution optical and radar datasets. The ‘doubly shadowed craters’ in the lunar permanently shadowed regions (PSRs) provide access to some of the coldest environments on the Moon that are ideal candidates for long-term volatile preservation. However, identifying subsurface ice unambiguously and translating these detections into actionable exploration strategies (landing and rover traversal) remains a key challenge.

Objective:
1. Identify and map potential subsurface ice bearing regions in the lunar south polar PSRs, with emphasis on ‘doubly shadowed craters’.
2. Utilize radar polarimetric signatures to distinguish ice-rich regions within ‘doubly shadowed craters’ from rough, rocky terrains.
3. Integrate terrain and illumination constraints to propose a scientifically viable and safe landing site near a ‘doubly shadowed crater’.
4. Design an optimal rover traverse path from the landing site to the target ‘doubly shadowed crater’.
5. Estimate the volume of subsurface ice within the top ~5 meters of lunar regolith at the identified ‘doubly shadowed crater’.

Expected Outcomes:
1. Identification of high-probability subsurface ice regions in ‘doubly shadowed craters’
2. A validated radar-based detection framework for subsurface ice
3. A feasible landing site near scientifically relevant targets
4. An optimized rover traverse path to access subsurface ice within ‘doubly shadowed crater’
5. Quantitative estimates of subsurface ice volume within ‘doubly shadowed crater’

Datasets Required: 
1. Chandrayaan-2 Dual Frequency Synthetic Aperture Radar (DFSAR)
2. Chandrayaan-2 Orbiter High Resolution Camera (OHRC)
The participants will be supplied with Chandrayaan-2 DFSAR data of a ‘doubly shadowed crater’ located in the lunar south polar region.

Suggested Tools / Technologies:
GIS platforms: QGIS / ArcGIS
Programming: Python (NumPy, SciPy, GDAL, rasterio)
Image processing: ENVI
DFSAR Processing: MIDAS
Terrain analysis: Digital Elevation Models (DEM tools)
Path planning: Optimization algorithms / AI-based navigation models
Visualization: QGIS / ArcGIS / MATLAB / Python-based plotting

Expected Workflows / Solutions:
1. Map permanently shadowed regions and identify ‘doubly shadowed craters’ using illumination models and OHRC imagery.
2. Analyze DFSAR data to compute radar parameters such as Circular Polarization Ratio (CPR) and Degree of Polarization (DOP). Apply refined criteria (e.g., CPR > 1 and DOP < 0.13) to identify potential subsurface ice signatures.
3. Study crater morphology, slopes, boulder distribution, and surface roughness using OHRC data.
4. Evaluate terrain safety and proximity to ice-bearing regions.
5. Design an optimal and safe path considering terrain hazards and solar power constraints.
6. Use radar backscatter models and dielectric assumptions to estimate ice concentration and volume within the top 5 meters.

Evaluation Parameters:
Scientific robustness of ice detection approach
Accuracy and clarity in data analysis and interpretation
Feasibility of proposed landing site
Efficiency and safety of rover traverse design
Innovation in methodology and tools used
Clarity of presentation and documentation

Subsequently, the participants have to design an optimal and safe rover traverse path to access subsurface ice in the ‘doubly shadowed crater’ considering terrain hazards and solar power constraints.

Implications of the Work:
This problem statement directly contributes to future lunar exploration missions led by ISRO by enabling:
1. Identification of in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) sites
2. Strategic planning for lunar landing missions
3. Improved understanding of subsurface ice distribution on the Moon
3. Advancement in planetary radar remote sensing techniques
The outcomes could play a crucial role in shaping future robotic and human exploration of the lunar South Pole.```
